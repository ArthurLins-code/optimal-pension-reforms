0 Start here — what this is, who it's for

This guide walks a brand-new RA through exactly what happens when you estimate the paper's headline number on your laptop, using the 5% anonymized sample. No prior knowledge of the codebase is assumed. By the end you will know which files run, in what order, what flows between them, and what the two hardest steps (the F-stage "bunching" counterfactual and the I-stage WMVPF) are actually computing — with worked arithmetic.

The single most important thing to understand first. The heavy data-construction stages A → B → C → D (linking two government datasets, cleaning them, imputing contribution time, building the panel) are NOT run on the sample. Those run only on the confidential server. The sample is the finished product of A–D: someone on the server took the final datasets, drew 5% of the people, replaced their national IDs with fake ones (id_0000001, id_0000002, …), baked in every derived column, and froze it as two flat CSV files. So on your laptop the pipeline starts at stage E, reading those two CSVs directly.

What you feed in

  • The sample data (external, read-only): dt_sampled_anon.csv (one row per retiree) and panel_sampled_anon.csv (~928 MB, one row per retiree-per-quarter).
  • One prereq input table that the sample can't regenerate, staged for you under PATHS$prereq_root: F5_table_results.csv only. (G4 / H2 are no longer external prereqs — they're regenerated in-repo by the G4 / H2 stages this run.) The run stops loudly if F5 is missing.

What comes out

  • Figures + tables for every stage, written inside the repo at analysis/output/ (gitignored).
  • The headline welfare numbers from I6: WMVPF of the actual reform, plus the pure-Level (bL) and pure-Slope (bS) decomposition.
  • A compiled 142-page LaTeX deck, _main.pdf.

1 Jargon in 60 seconds

Six words do most of the work in this project. Skim these once; they're all you need to follow the diagram and the worked examples.

points / points_norm — Brazil's pension eligibility uses "points" = your age + years of contribution. points_norm is your distance from the threshold (85 for women, 95 for men). 0 = exactly at the cutoff; below 0 = not yet there; above = past it.
counterfactual — the "what if the reform never happened" world. We never observe it, so the code constructs it. Everything downstream compares actual vs counterfactual.
hazard — a conditional exit rate. The claiming hazard = among people who are eligible but haven't claimed yet, the fraction that claims this quarter.
bunching — people cluster ("bunch") their retirement just past the cutoff, because crossing the threshold removes a benefit penalty. The spike of claims right above points_norm = 0 is bunching.
DD (difference-in-differences) — compares how an outcome moves for a group affected by the reform vs a group that isn't, before vs after. It isolates the reform's effect from background trends.
PDV (present discounted value) — a stream of future money expressed as one number today, discounting later payments. Every "benefit" here is a lifetime PDV in reais, not a monthly amount.
MVPF / WMVPF — (Weighted) Marginal Value of Public Funds = welfare delivered to people ÷ net cost to the government. A value in (0,1) means each R$1 spent buys less than R$1 of welfare.
η (eta) & γ (gamma) — γ=4 is society's risk aversion (CRRA); η≈0.828 is the welfare weight on a real given to a pension beneficiary vs the average citizen. η is computed from consumption levels, never retyped.

3 The whole pipeline, on one screen

Two inputs feed one command (analysis/analysis_all.R), which runs eight stages in order and writes everything into the repo; a second command (deck_tools/build_deck.R) turns those figures into the deck.

Why the split matters. The line between "inputs live outside the repo, outputs live inside the repo" is the whole mental model. You never edit or rebuild the inputs; you only ever regenerate the in-repo outputs. That's why analysis_all.R wipes only its temp folder and never the output folder or the prereq folder — deleting either would destroy files a later stage needs.

4 The two commands

From a terminal at the repo root. No setwd, no path editing — config/paths.R finds the project root via a .here sentinel and auto-detects DATA_MODE=sample because the sample root exists.

# 1 · run the analysis: panel + prereq tables → figures, tables, WMVPF
Rscript analysis/analysis_all.R

# 2 · collect the figures and compile the English deck (_main.pdf)
Rscript deck_tools/build_deck.R

Prefer R / RStudio? Open R at the repo root and source("RUN.R") — it's a signpost that prints the three masters — then source(here::here("analysis","analysis_all.R")) and the deck master.

What command 1 does, in order

It first checks the one prereq input (F5) exists (and stops loudly if not), then runs the eight stages:

E4gabrielpure G4G5H2 I4I6

The headline numbers come out of the last one, I6.

What is not run

build/build_all.R deliberately stop()s in sample mode — the panel is an input you already have, not something to rebuild. So A4 → B4 → C6 → D4 and their whole lineage never execute here. Also skipped: H3 (full-data only, no sample branch) and I7 / I7b (manual diagnostics, not on the master).

The i4-g4h2 quirk — now FIXED. Previously I4 read the full-data (no-suffix) G4_table_results.csv / H2_table_results.csv as prereq inputs, while I6 read the _sample-suffixed versions produced this run. In sample mode that mixed sample claim-counts with full-data benefits/taxes. I4 now reads the suffix-aware _sample G4/H2 from the in-repo output — exactly like I6 (file.path(PATHS$output_G, paste0('G4_table_results', SUFFIX, '.csv'))), so only F5 stays external. This shifted I4's sample WMVPF_actual from 0.2126 → 0.2106 (still in (0,1)); I6 is unchanged (0.2126). It's an econometric change pending professor review.

5 Stage by stage

One card per stage: the script, what flows in, what it does in plain words, what flows out, and — where relevant — an OBSCURE PART box explaining the one line a newcomer will stare at.

A–D build/code/ · A4 B4 C6 D4 (+ lineage) NOT RUN on the sample

On the server these link SUIBE (the pension-claim registry) to RAIS (the employment census), restrict to eligible workers, impute exact contribution time, and build the quarterly panel. On the sample there is nothing to do: the two CSVs you feed in are the frozen 5% slice of those outputs, with every derived column (points, distances to the cutoff, benefits, taxes, hazards) already baked in. If you're curious how A–D work, that's the server guide.

E4 E4_plots_claiming_distributions.R
Inputs →
dt_sampled_anon.csv, panel_sampled_anon.csv (read directly from the sample data/ folder).
Does
Draws diagnostic pictures of when and at what point-distance people claim their pensions — the raw claiming distributions that motivate everything after it. No estimation, just plots.
Outputs →
Claiming-distribution figures → analysis/output/E/. A few feed the deck.
F · gabriel new_counterfactual_claiming3_gabriel.R
Inputs →
F5_table_results.csv (prereq — holds the empirical claiming hazard ch and the DD-estimated reform effect on it per cell), plus the sample panel & cross-section.
Does
Builds the no-reform world quarter by quarter. It counts actual claims per cell (p,t) = (points-from-cutoff, quarters-since-reform), strips the reform's boost out of the hazard (ch_c = ch·(1−effect)), and walks each cohort forward to get counterfactual claims Nᶜ alongside actual claims Nᵃ.
Outputs →
new_counterfactual_claim_counts_sample.csv (→ G5, I4, I6) and the handoff file claims_actual_counterfactual_t_p_sample.csv (→ pure), plus PDFs. All under analysis/output/F/ and analysis/temp/.
OBSCURE PART — the "elig_c" recursion. The counterfactual isn't just "apply the counterfactual hazard to the observed pool." Because the reform made people claim early, in the no-reform world more people are still waiting in later quarters. The recursion carries that surplus forward — the extra counterfactual pool each quarter equals the running sum of (actual − counterfactual claims) so far. Cohorts march along diagonals (cohort = p − t/2) because points accrue 0.5 per quarter.
F · pure new_counterfactual_claiming3_pure.R canonical F
Inputs →
claims_actual_counterfactual_t_p_sample.csv (gabriel's handoff — the (p, t, Nᵃ, Nᶜ) table).
Does
Splits the reform's footprint into two one-lever-at-a-time hypothetical reforms: pure LEVEL (bL) and pure SLOPE (bS). It finds the "postponers" (cells where Nᶜ>Nᵃ below the cutoff), routes their mass to where they'll re-emerge just above the cutoff (bunching), and builds two mirror-image claim schedules claims_L and claims_S.
Outputs →
new_counterfactual_claim_counts_with_pure_schedules_3_sample.csv (→ G5, I6) + per-quarter PDFs → analysis/output/F/ and analysis/output/new_counter_claiming/.
OBSCURE PART — "proportional mixing." The postponers who bunch are spread across the window p ∈ [0,4) in the same proportions as observed actual claimers (a share profile g that sums to 1). Because g sums to 1, the bunching mass exactly equals the arriving mass — the decomposition conserves people: in every cell Nᴸ + Nˢ = Nᵃ + Nᶜ. Worked out step-by-step in §6.
G4 G4_effect_average_benefit_freq.R
Inputs →
dt_sampled_anon.csv (life-expectancy column already baked in).
Does
Computes the average lifetime benefit (as a PDV) per cell and runs an event-study DD to separate the real formula change from a composition shift (different people choosing to claim after the reform). It emits both the raw cell mean and the DD estimate side by side.
Outputs →
G4_table_results_sample.csv (→ I6) + event-study PDFs → analysis/output/G/.
OBSCURE PART — the "selection correction." Downstream stages compute delta_ben = avg_benefits_pv − point_estimate: subtract the DD estimate of the composition shift to leave the benefit a comparable claimer would draw. The ×3 in the PDV is "monthly → quarterly"; the annuity factor discounts the benefit over the retiree's remaining quarters of life. The far-below group [−15,−7] is the control (too far from the cutoff to re-time).
G5 G5_effect_average_benefit_freq_bL_and_bS.R
Inputs →
dt_sampled_anon.csv + pure's ...with_pure_schedules_3_sample.csv + economic constants.
Does
A superset of G4: repeats its DD, then builds benefit schedules for the two pure reforms (bL raises the replacement-rate level; bS flattens its slope), runs a second-round DD on each, and splits the effect into postponement vs anticipation pieces.
Outputs →
G5_table_results_selection_sample.csv and G5_table_results_contrafactual_reforms_and_benefits_freq_sample.csv (→ I6) → analysis/output/G/.
OBSCURE PART — a flagged assumption. The bL/bS benefit formulas divide by the pre-reform replacement rate, which the code's own comment calls a "potentially problematic assumption — verify against the canonical deck appendix." Treat it as an approximation, not an identity. (In the reverted strategy, I6 uses G5's benefit columns and the claim counts; the old frequency-space Beta/claims_L/S columns are dead.)
H2 H2_policy_elasticity_MW.R
Inputs →
dt_sampled_anon.csv + panel_sampled_anon.csv.
Does
The fiscal side: when the reform changes retirement timing, how much does government contribution (tax) revenue move? It compares workers more vs less exposed to the fator previdenciário penalty, reweights them to be comparable (IPW), and runs a DD on the year-over-year change in taxes.
Outputs →
H2_table_results_sample.csv (→ I6) + figures → analysis/output/H/.
OBSCURE PART — treatment is about the fator, not income. The <1.25MW group isn't "low earners" in a welfare sense — it's the group whose benefit was least reduced by the fator, so least incentivized to re-time. Getting that backwards flips the sign. IPW (inverse-probability weighting) is doing real identification work, not decoration: it forces the two groups to overlap on observables. Note the DD reference period here is −1, vs −2 in G4/G5.
I4 I4_wmvpf_no_pure_reforms_freq.R
Inputs →
Sample panel + cross-section; F counts new_counterfactual_claim_counts_sample.csv (with suffix); the _sample G4_table_results_sample.csv and H2_table_results_sample.csv produced this run in-repo (suffix-aware, exactly like I6).
Does
Builds three "worlds" of cumulative lifetime benefits — counterfactual b(x), mechanical b'(x), actual b'(x') — then divides welfare by net cost to get the WMVPF of the actual 2015 reform.
Outputs →
I4_table_wmvpf_sample.csv + I4_plot_results_sample.pdf → analysis/output/I/.
OBSCURE PART — two data routes for the same kind of number. b'(x') is summed over real individuals in the panel; b(x)/b'(x) can't be (those people/timings don't exist), so they're rebuilt from cell averages × counterfactual head-counts. Also: the H2 tax term is computed but commented out of the cost line, so the current WMVPF is benefit-side only. The full mechanics are in §7.
★ I6 I6_wmvpf_with_pure_reforms_freq.R headline
Inputs →
Everything I4 uses — including the same suffix-aware _sample G4/H2 produced this run (now shared with I4) — plus G5_table_results_contrafactual_reforms_and_benefits_freq_sample.csv.
Does
Part 1 reproduces I4's actual-reform WMVPF with one remaining fix (extends the horizon to t=13); the suffix-aware table reading is now shared with I4, so it's no longer an I6-only fix. Part 2 answers the policy question: of the reform's two levers, which delivers more welfare per real? It simulates the pure-Level and pure-Slope reforms in expenditure space and computes a WMVPF for each.
Outputs →
I6_wmvpf_actual_sample.csv, I6_wmvpf_pure_L_sample.csv, I6_wmvpf_pure_S_sample.csv, I6_summary_sample.csv + plots → analysis/output/I/.
OBSCURE PART — the bL vs bS sign seam. Both pure reforms share one reallocation box that moves postponers' expenditure from origin cells to bunching cells (scaled by the claiming probability g_pta, with the "2 quarters buys 1 point" geometry). The only difference is assembly: pure-Level adds it onto the counterfactual (Eᴸ = Eᶜ + Eᴾ); pure-Slope subtracts it from the actual (Eˢ = Eᵃ − Eᴾ). The code itself says "verify carefully" here.
The headline conclusions (full data). All three WMVPFs land in (0,1); WMVPF_bS > WMVPF_bL — the slope lever is more welfare-efficient than the level lever; so the budget-neutral improving move is raise bS, lower bL. The actual 2015 reform went the opposite way (raised level, cut slope). I6 prints this conclusion explicitly.
Don't panic at the sample's pure numbers. On the 5% sample, I6's actual WMVPF is a healthy ≈ 0.21 (in (0,1) ✓), but the pure decompositions come out outside (0,1) and even reversed (bL ≈ 1.56, bS ≈ −2.43). That is expected sampling noise on the small pure-decomposition cells — the full-data canonical values are ≈ 0.68 / 0.71 and satisfy bS > bL. The 5% sample is a pipeline-validation harness, not a replication; it also runs a provisional _multby20 ×20 blow-up just to eyeball the sample at population scale. None of this is a bug.

6 Toy example — the F-stage bunching counterfactual, with real arithmetic

This is the single hardest idea in the F stage. Made-up round numbers make it concrete. We follow one arrival cohort: everyone whose points cross the cutoff (p = 0) in quarter t = 4. Since points accrue 0.5 per quarter, moving p points to the cutoff takes 2p quarters, so this cohort's cells satisfy t = 4 + 2p.

Step 1 · Find the postponers (below the cutoff, p < 0)

Where the counterfactual exceeds the actual (Nᶜ > Nᵃ), the gap is people the reform induced to wait. Meet two of them: id_0000001 sits at p = −2 in quarter t = 0; id_0000002 sits at p = −1 in quarter t = 2. Their cells:

cell (p, t)whoNᵃ actualNᶜ counterfactualpostponers = max(Nᶜ−Nᵃ, 0)
(−2, 0)id_0000001's cell10013030
(−1, 2)id_0000002's cell12014020

Postponement Arrivals for this cohort: PA = 30 + 20 = 50 people who will reach the cutoff in t = 4 and bunch just past it.

Step 2 · The bunching band above the cutoff (0 ≤ p < 4), same cohort

These are the cells where the postponers re-emerge. We read the actual claimers there to learn the shape of the spike:

cell (p, t)Nᵃ actualNᶜ counterfactual
(0, 4)200180
(1, 6)10090
(2, 8)10095

Step 3 · The share profile g (proportional mixing)

Assume postponers bunch across p in the same proportions as the actual claimers. Denominator = 200 + 100 + 100 = 400:

# g must sum to 1 across the window g₀ = 200/400 = 0.50 g₁ = 100/400 = 0.25 g₂ = 100/400 = 0.25 → Σ g = 1.00 ✓

Step 4 · Spread the postponers into bunching mass PB = g · PA

PB₀ = 0.50 × 50 = 25 PB₁ = 0.25 × 50 = 12.5 PB₂ = 0.25 × 50 = 12.5 → Σ PB = 50 = PA ✓ (mass conserved)

Step 5 · The two pure-reform schedules

Rule: below the cutoff, LEVEL keeps actual and SLOPE keeps counterfactual; inside the band, LEVEL lays the bunchers onto the counterfactual (Nᶜ + PB) while SLOPE strips them out of the actual (Nᵃ − PB). The two are mirror images and always sum to Nᵃ + Nᶜ.

cellbranchclaims_L (Nᴸ, level/bL)claims_S (Nˢ, slope/bS)Nᴸ + Nˢ= Nᵃ + Nᶜ
(−2, 0)p<0100130230230 ✓
(−1, 2)p<0120140260260 ✓
(0, 4)0≤p<4180 + 25 = 205200 − 25 = 175380380 ✓
(1, 6)0≤p<490 + 12.5 = 102.5100 − 12.5 = 87.5190190 ✓
(2, 8)0≤p<495 + 12.5 = 107.5100 − 12.5 = 87.5195195 ✓

Reading it: the 50 postponers (id_0000001's 30 + id_0000002's 20) re-appear as 50 units of bunching, split 25 / 12.5 / 12.5. The LEVEL world adds that spike onto the counterfactual and keeps actual behavior below the cutoff; the SLOPE world removes the same spike from the actual and reverts to the counterfactual below. That mirror is exactly what lets I6 value the two levers separately.

7 Toy example — the I-stage WMVPF, with real arithmetic

Now the headline number. First the concept, then the exact arithmetic. Everything is a cumulative lifetime PDV in R$ (here, millions), summed quarter by quarter after the reform.

The three worlds (why there are three)

symbolnamebenefit formulaclaiming choicesplain words
b(x)counterfactualoldoldwhat would have been paid with no reform
b'(x)mechanicalnewoldnew formula, same people claiming at the same times (behavior frozen)
b'(x')actualnewnewwhat was actually paid — new formula AND people re-timing

Two differences carry all the economics: b'(x) − b(x) = Mechanical Effect (pure formula change, the part beneficiaries value); b'(x') − b'(x) = Fiscal Externality (extra cost from behavior change alone). Their sum is the Total (net) Cost.

Grounding b'(x') in individuals: at t=0 it is literally the sum of every post-reform claimant's PDV in the panel — e.g. id_0000001 contributes 60 and id_0000002 contributes 40 → they add 100 to b'(x')(t=0). The other two worlds can't be summed from real people (those choices/timings never happened), so they're rebuilt from cell-averages × counterfactual counts. That's the "two data routes" from I4's card.

Constants used, exactly as in the code: welfare weight ETA = 0.828 (γ=4); cost interest 1.005³ = 1.015075; welfare discount 0.995³ = 0.985075. The made-up cumulative inputs:

tb(x)b'(x)b'(x')
09096100
1200212220

Step 1 · net_cost = (b'(x') − b(x)) / 1.015075t

t=0: (100 − 90) / 1 = 10.00 t=1: (220 − 200) / 1.015075 = 20 / 1.015075 = 19.70 Σ net_cost = 29.70

Step 2 · mechanical gain = b'(x) − b(x) (what beneficiaries value)

t=0: 96 − 90 = 6 t=1: 212 − 200 = 12

Step 3 · welfare = 0.985075t · ETA · (b'(x) − b(x))

t=0: 1 × 0.828 × 6 = 4.968 t=1: 0.985075 × 0.828 × 12 = 9.788 Σ welfare = 14.756

Step 4 · WMVPF = Σ welfare / Σ net_cost

WMVPF = 14.756 / 29.70 = 0.497 → 0.497 ∈ (0,1) ✓

Reading it: this reform delivers about 50 cents of welfare per real of net fiscal cost. It's below 1 for two reasons: ETA < 1 (a real to beneficiaries is worth 0.828 of a real to the average citizen), and the fiscal externality — the gap between b'(x') (100 / 220) and b'(x) (96 / 212) — adds cost without adding beneficiary welfare. Remove the externality (set b'(x') = b'(x)) and the ratio would climb toward ≈ 0.82. That's the whole story of why a redistribution-heavy reform scores below 1.

One expected oddity. Total Cost can be negative at t=0 and that's fine, not a bug: postponement front-loads apparent savings because b'(x') is tiny at t=0 (few claim under the new schedule) while b(x) already carries a full old-schedule cohort.

8 Sample vs Server — the one-line difference

You just ran the sample path: two frozen CSVs in, two commands, outputs in-repo, numbers that validate the pipeline rather than replicate the paper. The server path is the same analysis stages preceded by the full A–D build on the confidential SUIBE/RAIS data, producing the real paper numbers.
