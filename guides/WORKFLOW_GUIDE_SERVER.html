0 What this is, and who it's for

The paper asks a single question: is Brazil's 2015 pension reform worth what it costs? The answer is a number — the WMVPF — and this pipeline is the machine that computes it from raw administrative data. If you have just joined the project and have server access, read this to understand what runs, in what order, and why.

Two Brazilian government datasets go in the front door. SUIBE is the pension-claim registry: who claimed a retirement benefit, when, and how large it was (2012–2019). RAIS is the formal-employment census: everyone's job history — wages, hours, sector, firm (1995–2020). The build phase links them person-by-person and reshapes them into two analysis-ready tables. The analysis phase estimates behaviour and welfare. The presentation phase turns the results into the canonical slide deck. On the server all of this runs on the full, confidential data; the 5% sample that runs on your laptop is carved out of the build output — see §6.

The one-line mental model. raw SUIBE + RAIS → build (A–D) → the panel → analysis (E–I) → WMVPF → deck. The panel file is the seam between build and analysis; everything upstream of it is server-only, everything downstream also runs on the sample.

2 The whole pipeline, on one page

Read it top to bottom. Raw admin data enters the build; the build writes the panel; the panel is the seam that both feeds analysis and (via a 5% draw) becomes the laptop sample; analysis writes figures and tables; the presentation master collects and compiles the deck.

3 Vocabulary — the jargon, in plain words

These terms recur everywhere below. Skim now; refer back as needed.

TermPlain meaning
Points (p, points_norm)Brazil's eligibility metric = age + years of contribution. The running variable is centred on the threshold: p = points − 85 (women) or − 95 (men). p<0 = below the sweet spot; p=0 = exactly at it; p>0 = past it. Points accrue at 0.5 per quarter.
Fator previdenciário (fp)An actuarial factor that shrinks your benefit if you claim "too early." fp<1 = a penalty; fp≥1 = no penalty. Crossing the points threshold makes the penalty vanish — which is why people cluster there.
Claiming hazardAmong people who are eligible but have not yet claimed, the fraction that claims this quarter. A "hazard" is a conditional exit rate.
Counterfactual"What would have happened without the reform." The F-stage constructs the no-reform world quarter by quarter, because it cannot be observed.
Bunching / postponementBunching = a pile-up of claims just past the threshold. Postponement = people just below the threshold waiting to cross it. The reform pushed people to postpone, then bunch.
DD (difference-in-differences) / event studyCompare the change over time in a treated group vs. a control group. An event study reports one DD coefficient per quarter-relative-to-the-reform, normalised to a reference quarter.
Selection correctionAfter the reform, different people claim in a given cell, so the raw average benefit moves even if the formula didn't. Subtracting the DD "composition" coefficient (delta_ben = avg_benefits_pv − point_estimate) removes that and leaves the mechanical benefit.
PDV (present discounted value)A monthly benefit converted to a lifetime value in today's R$: 3 · benefit · annuity_factor (×3 = months per quarter; annuity factor discounts the stream over remaining life at 6%/yr).
MVPF / WMVPFWeighted Marginal Value of Public Funds = (welfare delivered to beneficiaries) ÷ (net fiscal cost). A value in (0,1) means each R$1 spent buys less than R$1 of welfare.
ME / FE / TCMechanical Effect = cost of the new formula with behaviour frozen. Fiscal Externality = extra cost purely from behaviour changing. Total Cost = ME + FE.
η (ETA)The welfare weight on a real given to a beneficiary: 1 − γ·(c_INSS − c_POP)/c_POP ≈ 0.828 (γ=4). Below 1 because beneficiaries consume slightly more than the average citizen.
IPW (inverse-probability weighting)Reweight treated/control groups by 1/propensity so their observable characteristics line up — used in H2 to make the tax comparison quasi-random.

4 Build stages A–D — raw data becomes the panel

The build runs only on the server (build/build_all.R stop()s unless DATA_MODE=full). The master runs four canonical heads — A4 → B4 → C6 → D4 — but a from-scratch rebuild needs the upstream lineage first (A1–A3, B1–B3, C1–C5, D1–D3) to fill the working/ intermediates the heads consume. The real data spine is A1→A3 · B4 → C1 → C4/C5 → C6 → D3 → D4; A4 is a dead-end diagnostic that feeds nobody.

Stage A  Clean & merge SUIBE (the claim registry)

Inputs → raw SUIBE Excels (2012–19; only 2012/2013 carry a full CPF), IBGE life-expectancy table.

Does A1–A2 parse and clean the "semi-identified" and unidentified SUIBE streams (birth date, sex, claim/issue dates, fator_prev); A3 stacks them into one claimant table and attaches life expectancy. A4 is a balance check, not construction.

Outputs → working/A3_merged_suibe.csv.gz; A4 → A/A4_balance_check.csv/.tex.

Obscure part — what "balance check" means. A4 builds d_unique (a row is "unique" only if its claim-date/issue-date/birth-date/male tuple appears exactly once, via a double duplicated()), then regresses ~18 covariates on it. The question it answers: are the people we can pin down demographically the same kind of people as the ones we can't? If yes, the record-linkage model (C1) trained on the pin-down-able people generalises to everyone.

Stage B  Build the RAIS feature cross-section (the match candidates)

Inputs → raw RAIS yearly .dta, A3_merged_suibe, INPC price index, CNAE/CBO crosswalks, IBGE municipality map.

Does B1–B3 (Stata) keep people born ≤1973, denoise each person's ID (mode of CPF across years → CPF_mode), and write RAIS history per year. B4 turns that history into ~16 modeled features per person for each claim-year 2010–2019, using only the 15 years before the claim.

Outputs → working/B4_clean_candidates_cross/B4_<year>.csv.gz.

Obscure part — the "modal category" idiom & employment probability. A terse data.table one-liner (…[,.(freq=.N),by=.(CPF_mode,cat)]…mult='last') appears a dozen times; it just means take the mode — the most frequent category per person. And prob_empl_15 expands each contract into 12 monthly employed/not flags and divides by 180 — i.e. "what fraction of the last 15 years was this person formally employed."

Stage C  Record linkage · restriction · contribution imputation (the intellectual core)

Inputs → A3_merged_suibe + the B4 candidate features; later, IBGE life expectancy.

Does C1 is the crown jewel: only ~19% of SUIBE claimants carry a full CPF, so for the other 81% a logit model decides which RAIS worker is the same human. C4 adds race/earnings/salario de beneficio; C5 restricts to the study population (claimed ≥2012; women ≥30 / men ≥35 contribution years; prob_empl_15 ≥ 0.4) → ~1,086,742 individuals; C6 imputes the continuous contribution time (SUIBE only reports whole-year bins).

Outputs → working/C1_merged_suibe_rais.csv.gz, C5_restricted_sample.csv.gz, C6_estimated_contrib_time.csv.gz.

Obscure part — C1's five-step probabilistic match. (1) exact-merge the 19% with full CPF = ground truth; (2) build a fuzzy blocking key fake_id (sex + birthdate + 3 CPF digits) to generate candidate pairs; (3) train a logit on the ground truth to predict "same person?" from agreement features (same region at 2/5/8/10/15-yr horizons, salary-vs-benefit quartile, …); (4) keep only mutually best-ranked pairs (one-to-one); (5) accept above a cutoff of 0.4953, chosen so the false-match rate ≈ 4%. Type-I = "declared a wrong match"; Type-II = "missed a real match." They tuned for few wrong matches (~4%) at the cost of missing ~16% of true ones.

Stage D  The two analysis-ready datasets

Inputs → C5_restricted_sample + C6_estimated_contrib_time + IBGE life expectancy; D4 also reads the filtered RAIS (C3), INPC, crosswalks.

Does D3 derives every reform-relevant variable per person: points_claim = age + contr_time_est, the signed distance to the 85/95 (or 86/96 in 2019) thresholds, the post-reform dummy (claim ≥ 2015-06-17), and the estimated fator fp_est. D4 reconstructs monthly earnings from RAIS, computes monthly tax = payroll (20%) + SS contribution + income tax (the government-revenue side of the MVPF), and builds a balanced quarterly panel on two clocks — a reform clock and a claim clock — plus the claiming hazard.

Outputs → working/D3_cross_section.csv.gz, working/D4_panel_reform.csv.gz, working/D4_panel_claim.csv.gz. These are the seam.

Obscure parts. (a) A silent num == 3 filter drops any quarter missing a month — person-quarters vanish with no comment. (b) Two panels, not one: "reform" and "claim" are the same data aligned to two different time-zeros; downstream DD picks whichever clock it needs. (c) The identifier is indiv (D3 renames CPF_mode → indiv).

5 The build → analysis seam (and the U:/ vs F:/ split)

Build and analysis meet at exactly two files: D3_cross_section.csv.gz and D4_panel_reform.csv.gz. Everything the analysis needs about a person — points, cutoff distances, fp_est, quarterly earnings, taxes, benefits, hazards — is already baked into these two tables. Nothing upstream is re-derived downstream.

The crossed-drive trap (open issue O11). The full-data build writes its working/ intermediates under U:/Documents/Paper/directory_2025, but the full-data analysis scripts are rooted under F:/Users/tucalins/…/directory_2025. config/paths.R centralises these as two named roots (FULL_BUILD_ROOT vs FULL_ANALYSIS_ROOT) but deliberately does not merge them — it is flagged for the professors to reconcile on the server. Practical consequence: on the server the D-stage outputs on U:/ must be visible to analysis code rooted on F:/. This cross-drive seam is the single most likely thing to break a naive full-data rerun — which is exactly why the everyday workflow is the two-CSV sample instead.

6 Where the 5% sample comes from

There is no sample-drawing script in this repo. The sample is created off-repo, on the server, directly from the build output, in four steps:

  1. Draw 5% of the CPFs from the D-stage outputs.
  2. Anonymize the identifier — the indiv/CPF_mode value is replaced by a sequential surrogate id_0000001, id_0000002, … (the column keeps the name indiv).
  3. Bake in every derived column (points, distances, fp_est, quarterly earnings, taxes, benefits, hazards) so nothing needs re-deriving and nothing confidential survives.
  4. Export two flat CSVs: dt_sampled_anon.csv (the 5% cross-section, analog of D3) and panel_sampled_anon.csv (the 5% quarterly panel, analog of D4).

Those two files live in the external sample root and are never committed. Because they already are the D-stage output, the analysis pipeline reads them directly and skips A–D entirely in sample mode — which is why build_all.R and every build head stop() when DATA_MODE ≠ full. The build block and the analysis block meet only at these two CSVs. See the sibling laptop-sample guide.

Honest caveat. The sample is a validation harness, not a replication. Its headline numbers differ from the paper by design (5% actual counts get mixed with full-data counterfactual frequencies) — the sample can even yield a negative WMVPF. That is expected sampling noise, not a bug.

7 Analysis stages E–I — behaviour, benefits, and the WMVPF

On the server, analysis/analysis_all.R runs in full mode and executes eight stages in order: E4 → gabriel → pure → G4 → G5 → H2 → I4 → I6 (plus H3, which becomes runnable only on full data). It reads the build's panel from F:/, plus the pre-supplied prereq table (F5 only — G4/H2 are now produced in-repo by the G4/H2 stages) and IBGE life tables.

E4  Claiming-distribution diagnostics

Inputs → the panel + cross-section.

Does draws diagnostic figures of how claims distribute around the threshold. No estimation.

Outputs → figures into analysis/output/E/.

F · gabriel  Build actual vs counterfactual claim counts

Inputs → F5_table_results.csv (empirical claiming hazards + the DD-estimated reform effect per cell), the panel.

Does counts actual claims Nᵃ per (p,t) cell; strips the reform's hazard boost to get the no-reform hazard ch_c = ch·(1−effect); then walks each diagonal cohort forward, carrying the surplus at-risk pool that the reform's early claiming left behind, to produce counterfactual claims Nᶜ.

Outputs → new_counterfactual_claim_counts.csv (Nᵃ, Nᶜ; read by I4/I6/G5) and the handoff claims_actual_counterfactual_t_p.csv that pure reads.

Obscure part — the eligible-pool recursion. The counterfactual is not just "apply the no-reform hazard to the observed pool." Because the reform made people claim early, in the no-reform world more people are still waiting later. The recursion carries that surplus forward: elig_c[i] = elig[i] + (claims[i-1] − claims_c[i-1]) − (elig[i-1] − elig_c[i-1]). Also note F5 is a legacy-produced table (flag g-f5) but is the accepted live input.

F · pure  Pure-Level (bL) and pure-Slope (bS) reform frequencies

Inputs → gabriel's claims_actual_counterfactual_t_p.csv (Nᵃ, Nᶜ per cell).

Does the 2015 reform bundled two levers (a higher benefit level and a flatter slope). Pure manufactures two clean one-lever worlds. It finds each person's arrival quarter (t_arrival = t − 2p), sums the postponers below the cutoff into arrival cohorts, distributes them across the bunching window using an empirical claim-share profile g (which sums to 1), and lays the resulting bunching mass PB onto the counterfactual.

Outputs → new_counterfactual_claim_counts_with_pure_schedules_3.csv (Nᴸ, Nˢ; read by G5 and I6).

Obscure part — the mirror identity. Nᴸ keeps actual behaviour below the cutoff and lays bunchers on the counterfactual just above it; Nˢ is the mirror. In every cell Nᴸ + Nˢ = Nᵃ + Nᶜ — the decomposition conserves mass. Worked out step by step in §9.

G4  Selection-corrected average benefits

Inputs → D1_cross_section (full mode) + IBGE life expectancy.

Does reconstructs both benefit schedules per claimant (old vs new, via fp_est), turns them into lifetime PDVs, collapses to cell means, and runs an event-study DD (reference quarter −2) with the far-below [-15,-7] window as control. Emits the raw cell mean and the DD coefficient side by side so consumers can compute delta_ben = avg_benefits_pv − point_estimate.

Outputs → output/G/G4_table_results.csv — the key input for I4.

Obscure part — why subtract the DD coefficient. A cell's average benefit moves after the reform for two reasons: the formula changed (mechanical) and different people now claim there (composition). The DD, measured against a control group that has no incentive to re-time, isolates the composition shift; subtracting it leaves the mechanical benefit that I4/I6 multiply by claim counts.

G5  DD on benefits + pure-reform schedules (bL / bS)

Inputs → the same cross-section + life table, plus pure's …with_pure_schedules_3.csv.

Does a superset of G4: it also builds the two pure-reform benefit schedules (a linear replacement-rate approximation — bL raises the intercept, bS flattens the slope), runs a second-round DD on each, splits the effect into postponement vs anticipation, and assembles quarterly mechanical/behavioral flows.

Outputs → output/G/G5_table_results_contrafactual_reforms_and_benefits_freq.csv — I6's pure-reform input.

Obscure part / flag. The bL/bS ratio formula divides by the pre-reform replacement rate — the code's own comment calls this a "potentially problematic assumption; verify against the canonical deck appendix." Treat it as an approximation. Also note G5 still reads D1 while its canonical peers read D3 (flag g5-d1).

H2  Tax/revenue policy elasticity (the fiscal term)

Inputs → D1_cross_section + D2_panel (full mode).

Does asks how much government contribution revenue moves when the reform changes retirement timing. Treatment = exposure to the fator (benefit salary not <1.25MW); IPW reweights treated/control to common support; the outcome is the year-over-year change in contributions; an event-study DD (reference −1) gives the elasticity, reported both plain and IPW-weighted.

Outputs → output/H/H2_table_results.csv — the revenue term for I4/I6.

Obscure part / flag. The treatment cutoff is about the fator, not income — <1.25MW is the group whose benefit was least reduced (hence least incentivised to re-time); getting this backwards flips the sign. And note CLAUDE.md calls H3 canonical, but H2 is the file that actually runs and feeds I4/I6 (flag h2-vs-h3). H3 is full-data-only, figures-only, on no master.

I4  WMVPF of the actual reform

Inputs → the panel; F-stage claim counts (with _sample suffix); G4 & H2 tables read suffix-aware from the in-repo output/ — now consistent with I6, so F5 is the only external input (the old i4-g4h2 quirk is fixed).

Does builds three worlds — b(x) counterfactual (old formula, old choices), b'(x) mechanical (new formula, old choices), b'(x') actual (new formula, new choices) — as cumulative PDVs per quarter. Then net_cost = b'(x')−b(x), welfare = 0.9953t·η·(b'(x)−b(x)), and wmvpf = Σ welfare / Σ net_cost.

Outputs → output/I/I4_table_wmvpf.csv, I4_plot_results.pdf.

Obscure parts / flags. (a) Two data routes: b'(x') is summed from individuals in the panel; b(x)/b'(x) are rebuilt from cell means × counterfactual counts (those people/claim-times don't exist to sum). (b) The i4-g4h2 quirk — now FIXED: G4/H2 used to be read full-data even in sample mode; I4 now reads them suffix-aware from the in-repo output/, exactly like I6, leaving F5 as the only external input. This moved I4's sample WMVPF_actual 0.2126 → 0.2106 (still in (0,1); I6 unchanged at 0.2126) — the econometric change is pending professor review. (c) The tax term is computed but commented out of net_cost — the current WMVPF is benefit-side only. (d) Cost vs welfare discounts (1.005³ vs 0.995³) are not exact reciprocals — a flagged, unharmonised seam.

I6  WMVPF + pure L/S decomposition ★ the headline

Inputs → everything I4 uses (but suffix-aware G4/H2, fixing the quirk), plus G5's contrafactual table.

Does Part 1 replicates I4's actual-reform WMVPF with two fixes (horizon extended to t=13; suffix-aware tables). Part 2 asks the policy question: of the two levers, which delivers more welfare per real? It simulates the pure-Level (bL) and pure-Slope (bS) reforms in expenditure space, physically reallocating postponers from origin cells to bunching cells via the claiming probability g_pta, and computes a WMVPF for each.

Outputs → I6_wmvpf_actual.csv, I6_wmvpf_pure_L.csv, I6_wmvpf_pure_S.csv, I6_summary.csv, plots.

Headline results (full data). All three WMVPFs land in (0,1) — the reform costs the government more than beneficiaries' willingness-to-pay. WMVPFbS > WMVPFbL — the slope lever is more welfare-efficient. The budget-neutral improving move is therefore raise bS, lower bL — the opposite of what the 2015 reform actually did.
Obscure part — the bL vs bS sign seam. Both pure reforms use the same postponement-reallocation box; they differ only in assembly: Eᴸ = Eᶜ + Eᴾ (add postponement onto the counterfactual) but Eˢ = Eᵃ − Eᴾ (subtract it from the actual). The code itself says "verify carefully." Also: TC<0 at t=0 is expected, not a bug (postponement front-loads apparent savings).

8 Presentation — collecting figures and compiling the deck

After analysis, presentation/build_deck.R runs three shell steps in sequence (any nonzero exit → stop()):

1 · collect

collector.py reads manifest.csv (72 figures) and, for each, resolves the source across search roots (taking the newest by mtime), then copies + renames the long code-output name to the short name the deck expects, into from_code/.

2 · compile

latexmk -cd -g -pdf … _main.tex compiles the Beamer deck. The \graphicspath searches from_code/ first, then static/ (hand-made / external assets).

3 · verify

verify_deck.py regex-scans _main.tex and confirms every \includegraphics resolves under from_code/ or static/. Result: _main.pdf, 142 pages.

The fragile coupling. The manifest's code_output_name must stay byte-identical to the filenames the R stages actually ggsave() — there is no fuzzy matching. If someone renames a plot in pure.R or G5, the collector silently reports it MISSING and the deck figure goes stale. (This is why the misspelled benegits in some G5 filenames is left as-is — it must match.)

9 Toy example — the counterfactual & bunching (F-stage)

Meet two workers. id_0000001 sits just below the cutoff (p<0) in an early quarter; the reform makes them wait — a postponer. id_0000002 has already crossed the cutoff; they claim in the bunching band (p≥0). On the full data each (p,t) cell holds thousands of such people; here we use tiny round counts so the arithmetic is visible. We follow one arrival cohort a = 4 — everyone who reaches p=0 in quarter t=4. All cells on that diagonal satisfy t = a + 2p (points accrue 0.5/quarter).

1 Postponers below the cutoff — where the arrivals come from (p<0, arrival cohort 4)

cell (p, t)whoNᵃ (actual)Nᶜ (counterfactual)postponers = max(Nᶜ−Nᵃ, 0)
(p=−2, t=0)id_0000001 & peers10013030
(p=−1, t=2)peers12014020

Postponement arrivals: PAa=4 = 30 + 20 = 50 — the mass that will reach the cutoff in quarter 4.

2 The bunching band above the cutoff — the claim-share profile (p≥0, arrival cohort 4)

cell (p, t)whoNᵃ (actual)Nᶜg = Nᵃ / Σ Nᵃ
(p=0, t=4)id_0000002 & peers200180200/400 = 0.50
(p=1, t=6)peers10090100/400 = 0.25
(p=2, t=8)peers10095100/400 = 0.25

Denominator = 200+100+100 = 400. The shares g sum to 1 ✓ — this is the "proportional mixing" assumption: postponers bunch in the same proportions as observed actual claimers.

3 Distribute the arrivals across the band — postponement bunching PB = g · PA

PB₀ = 0.50·50 = 25  ·  PB₁ = 0.25·50 = 12.5  ·  PB₂ = 0.25·50 = 12.5  →  Σ PB = 50 = PA ✓ (mass conserved)

4 The two pure-reform frequencies — and the conservation check

Rule: below the cutoff Nᴸ = Nᵃ, Nˢ = Nᶜ; inside the band Nᴸ = Nᶜ + PB, Nˢ = Nᵃ − PB.

cell (p, t)branchNᴸ (pure LEVEL / bL)Nˢ (pure SLOPE / bS)Nᴸ+Nˢ = Nᵃ+Nᶜ ?
(−2, 0)p<0Nᵃ = 100Nᶜ = 130230 = 100+130 ✓
(−1, 2)p<0120140260 = 120+140 ✓
(0, 4)0≤p<4180 + 25 = 205200 − 25 = 175380 = 200+180 ✓
(1, 6)0≤p<490 + 12.5 = 102.5100 − 12.5 = 87.5190 = 100+90 ✓
(2, 8)0≤p<495 + 12.5 = 107.5100 − 12.5 = 87.5195 = 100+95 ✓

Reading it: the 50 postponers (id_0000001's crowd) re-emerge as 50 units of bunching mass, split 0.50/0.25/0.25. The LEVEL world adds that bunching on top of the counterfactual just past the cutoff and keeps actual behaviour below it; the SLOPE world removes the same bunching from the actual and reverts to the counterfactual below. The two always sum back to actual + counterfactual.

10 Toy example — the WMVPF (I-stage)

id_0000001 and id_0000002 are two retirees whose lifetime PDV benefits accumulate into the cumulative b-values below. On full data these are sums over ~1.08M people, each in the hundreds of thousands of R$; here we use R$ millions, round, over just two quarters. Recall the three worlds: b(x) = old formula/old choices, b'(x) = new formula/old choices (mechanical), b'(x') = new formula/new choices (actual). Constants: η = 0.828, cost factor 1.005³ = 1.015075, welfare factor 0.995³ = 0.985075.

1 The inputs (cumulative PDV, R$M)

tb(x)b'(x) mechanicalb'(x') actual
09096100
1200212220

2 Net cost = (b'(x') − b(x)) / 1.015075t

t=0: (100 − 90)/1 = 10.00  ·  t=1: (220 − 200)/1.015075 = 20/1.015075 = 19.70  →  Σ net_cost = 29.70

3 Mechanical gain (what beneficiaries value) = b'(x) − b(x)

t=0: 96 − 90 = 6  ·  t=1: 212 − 200 = 12

4 Welfare = 0.985075t · 0.828 · (mechanical gain)

t=0: 1 · 0.828 · 6 = 4.968  ·  t=1: 0.985075 · 0.828 · 12 = 9.788  →  Σ welfare = 14.756

5 WMVPF = Σ welfare / Σ net_cost

WMVPF = 14.756 / 29.70 = 0.497   ∈ (0,1) ✓

Reading it: this reform delivers ~50 cents of welfare per real of net fiscal cost. It is below 1 for two reasons: η < 1 (a real to a beneficiary is worth 0.828 of a real to the average citizen), and the fiscal externality — the gap between b'(x') and b'(x) (people re-timing) adds cost without adding beneficiary welfare. Remove the externality (set b'(x') = b'(x)) and the ratio climbs toward η.

Full-data note. These are toy magnitudes. On the real reform the b-values are lifetime PDVs summed over ~1.08M restricted-sample individuals, and the headline I6 WMVPFs all land in (0,1) with bS > bL. The 5% sample reproduces the mechanics exactly but not the magnitudes — see §11.

12 Running it on the server, and the gotchas

Set the mode once, then run the three masters in order. config/paths.R finds the repo root via the .here sentinel — no setwd, no path editing.

Shell / batch job

export PENSION_DATA_MODE=full
Rscript build/build_all.R         # raw SUIBE/RAIS → panel (U:/); stops unless full
Rscript analysis/analysis_all.R   # panel → figures, tables, WMVPF (F:/)
Rscript presentation/build_deck.R # figures → _main.pdf (142 pages)

From scratch: build_all.R runs the canonical heads A4/B4/C6/D4; run the upstream lineage (A1–A3, B1–B3, C1–C5, D1–D3) first. Stata steps: stata-mp -b do build/code/<file>.do.

From R / RStudio

Sys.setenv(PENSION_DATA_MODE = "full")
source(here::here("build","build_all.R"))
source(here::here("analysis","analysis_all.R"))
source(here::here("presentation","build_deck.R"))

RUN.R at the repo root is the front-door signpost listing the three masters.
